Dailu Guan received a Bachelor's degree in Animal Science and a Master's degree in Animal Genetics and Breeding from Southwest University. He was then trained in quantitative genetics in the Autonomous University of Barcelona under the supervision of Prof. Marcel Amills in 2020. In 2021, he joined Huaijun Zhou's group at UC Davis as a postdoctoral researcher, where he was trained in computational epigenomics. He currently holds the position of Reserch Scientist in the Department of Animal Science at the University of California, Davis.

Research Interests

I specialize in the field of computational and quantitative genomics, dedicating my research to dissecting the genetic and epigenetic architecture of complex agricultural traits. My specific research aims include identifying causal variants, genes, and pathways underlying complex traits within tissue, cellular, and genomic native contexts through the combination of state-of-the-art multi-omics datasets (e.g., WGS, RNA-seq, ChIP-seq, ATAC-seq, RRBS, WGBS, Hi-C) and cutting-edge methodologies (e.g., genome-wide association studies, deep learning). My research will deepen our understanding of the vast spectrum of complex traits in humans, animals, and plants, promising knowledge discovery and advancing agricultural practices. Ultimately, this research is positioned to equip us with the knowledge and tools needed to navigate and manipulate the complex genetic foundations of the traits that shape our world.

Complex Traits Genetics

The central dogma of molecular biology suggests that DNA sequence variations affect phenotypes through transcription and translation, mediated by complex layers of multi-omic regulation. Annotating functional genomic elements and understanding how variants influence these elements are crucial for linking phenotype-associated variants with gene regulatory mechanisms. My research will focus on decoding the linkages between sequence variants and multi-omics regulation (e.g. chromatin accessibility, histone modification, gene expression, protein abundance) and observable phenotype expression, as well as their interactions with environmental factors.

Methodology Development

The decrease in sequencing costs has led to an accumulation of genomics and epigenomics data to decipher the genome-to-phenome relationship. However, these data often remain sparse or low-quality due to financial constraints and their complex dimensions. My research focuses on developing methodologies to address the multi-dimensionality of incoming data and manage missing and low-quality data to enhance complex traits genetics. By leveraging cutting-edge methods such as deep learning algorithms, I aim to impute missing data and transfer insights across species. These innovations will enhance genome annotation and shed light on phenotypic variations across various contexts, including species, tissues, and developmental stages.
