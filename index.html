Dailu Guan received a Bachelor's degree in Animal Science and a Master's degree in Animal Genetics and Breeding from Southwest University. He was then trained in quantitative genetics in the Autonomous University of Barcelona under the supervision of Prof. Marcel Amills in 2020. In 2021, he joined Huaijun Zhou's group at UC Davis as a postdoctoral researcher, where he was trained in computational epigenomics. He currently holds the position of Reserch Scientist in the Department of Animal Science at the University of California, Davis.

Research Interests

I specialize in the field of computational and quantitative genomics, dedicating my research to dissecting the genetic and epigenetic architecture of complex agricultural traits in farm animals. My specific research aims include identifying causal variants, genes, and pathways underlying complex traits within tissue, cellular, and genomic native contexts through the combination of state-of-the-art multi-omics data (e.g., WGS, RNA-seq, ChIP-seq, ATAC-seq, RRBS, WGBS, Hi-C) and cutting-edge methodologies (e.g., genome-wide association studies, deep learning). My research will deepen our understanding of the vast spectrum of complex traits, promising knowledge discovery and advancing agricultural practices. Ultimately, my research is positioned to equip us with the knowledge and tools needed to genetically improve agricultural traits with economic value in food animal populations.

Complex Traits Genetics

Complex traits in farm animals arise from the interplay of multiple genetic and environmental factors, making their study essential for improving economically valuable traits. The central dogma of molecular biology suggests that DNA sequences shape phenotypes through a cascade of processes involving transcription, translation, and regulation. My research seeks to understand how sequence variations influence multi-omics layers—such as chromatin accessibility, histone modifications, gene expression, and protein abundance—and how these layers contribute to observable phenotypes. By investigating these interactions within and across species, I aim to elucidate the regulatory mechanisms driving complex traits and their responses to diverse environmental conditions to enhance breeding strategies and support sustainable farm animal development.
